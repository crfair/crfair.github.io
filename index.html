Taste of Culture

"Taste of Culture" was a school project made during Fall Semester 2021 at RIT. I, along with my team Too Many Cooks, developed this game from September through December 2021 for IGME 601, Game Development Processes, a class which was about analysing the processes that team can use to effectively build a game and putting Agile philosophies into practice.

I was one of two technical leads on this project, which meant that I handled a lot of the programming work and acted as an advisor for my 5 teammates when technical questions came up. More specifically, I was the primary developer of the dialogue system, general scene scripting and transitions, implementing the gif tutorials, managing the web build, and taking care of general polish and bug fixing.

Due to the focus of the class on processes, we were given some constraints in order to encourage us to consider our work efficiency, and we were also provided with an external stakeholder who provided additional requirements to be met. Our theme requirement was to create a game that promoted diversity; to that end, Taste of Culture strove to use food as a common ground: people of all cultures have an intimate but very culturally unique relationship with food, and cooking and eating is often a social activity over which bonding and understanding can happen. Our first level focused on Indian food, and further levels would focus on other cultures such as Korean, Carribean, Mexican, and many more. The other major constraints were that the project must be made in Unity, must have a web build, and each team member may spend no more than 3-4 hours a week working on the game. This significantly restrained the scope we would be able to implement, but in the end, we felt that we made a polished, mechanically sound game and I personally enjoyed the learning experience and am proud of the result.

Taste of Culture's GitHub repo can be found here, and the web build can be found here.

Season Soir√©e

"Seaon Soir√©e" was a rapid prototype assignment made during the Spring Semester 2022 at RIT. In this rapid prototyping course, teams are formed randomly and then given 2 weeks to design and develop a game meeting certain specifications.

I took the role of team lead during this project, which meant that I acted as the design lead and I identified, prioritized, and delegated tasks to my team members based on their skillsets and work preferences. I also was the primary level designer and I participated in gameplay programming, primarily for the obstacles that the player can encounter and for an event system so that actors could react to the season-changing mechanic.

The specifications given for this game included a working main menu and pause menu, an interesting theme, an unusual character that related to the theme, 3 different kinds of mobile actors, 3 different kinds of environmental effects, a level with multiple parts, and the game should be a platformer. This game's theme was seasons, and the aesthetics and the lore of the game were inspired by the Nordic legend of Yggdrasil and Mimir's Well. I leaned into the genre of puzzle platformer, mostly because of my preference for solving and designing puzzles, and with our unusual main character of a water droplet, he also has the unusual power to change form by changing the season between summer and winter. The environment changes along with him, though, so the player must be mindful of how their surroundings might change as well. Originally, we planned on having a limited amount of season changes that the player may make as well; time limits prevented this from being prioritized, but the player would've been encouraged to strategize carefully and inspect their surroundings before changing seasons. Though this game was only a 2 week prototype, I'm very proud of the leadership and design skills I was given the opportunity to exercise.

The repository containing this project's codebase can be found here.

Beauty

"Beauty" is a biological-technological art installation created by Dr. Carlos Castellanos, an artist, researcher, and professor at RIT. It has been in development since 2020, and I've been working on the project as a graduate research assistant since September 2021.

As a graduate research assistant, I have weekly meetings with Dr. Castellanos regarding his vision for the project, the immediate next direction to take, and what technologies and methods I should look into using for this project. Primarily, I focus on computer vision and basic machine learning techniques, including OpenCV and openFrameworks, StyleGAN2, and OSC. I implement these techniques in C++ and Python using Visual Studio and Google Colab, respectively.

This project is still in progress. The final vision of the project has yet to be determined or realized, but some projects I've worked on towards the development of this project include performing background subtraction and contour detection on images of bacteria using methods such as Canny and k Nearest Neighbor, Delaunay triangulation and Voronoi diagram generation on these contours, sending messages via OSC, and model training and latent space exploration with StyleGAN2.

The website detailing Beauty's previous installments can be found here, and the GitHub repo containing my person work can be found here.

Watch Dog

"Watch Dog" was a 2 semester project developed as part of my computer engineering capstone requirement for my undergrad at CNU. I worked with a team of 4 other students to develop a prototype product that could conceivably be marketed as an off-the-shelf product.

My role on the team was primarily as the front-end and database developer, as well as managing development for the data pipeline that would interface with either of these two things. I built a simple SQL server that would take in soome basic data from two sources, as well as an ASP.NET web app that would manage the reading and writing of this data. Additionally, since our prospective client was a local SPCA which I had volunteered for in the past, I acted as a liaison between our team and the shelter for determining customer needs, problems that could be solved by our product, arranging meetings for testing and collecting data, and providing updates so that we could receive feedback.

The goal of Watch Dog was to remove some of the strain that dog shelters experience when they have a moderate number of rotating staff and volunteers: important information about the dogs, including behavioral patterns that could indicate potential illness, often falls through the cracks as it is extremely difficult to disseminate information about a large number of dogs to a large number of people. Illnesses such as kennel cough are extremely common in dog shelters as a result of this difficulty of noticing patterns and catching diseases early. Our proposed solution involved recording the dogs 24/7 using a small Raspberry Pi camera, sending the videos to a machine learning algorithm that could recognize the dogs' body parts and movement, and send this information to a database that displayed it on an internal web application. Shelther staff and volunteers would also be able to create and edit additional information about the dogs besides the movement data, so the app acted as a general database as well. The vision of the project was to eventually have the algorithm recognize and classify certain animal behaviors, such as excessive scratching, as cause for concern, such that the shelter staff could quickly identify and mitigate illness in the dogs. This project taught me a lot about working with external stakeholders and I'm proud of the work I did developing the interface while working with the shelter to understand the wants and needs of our customers.

The GitHub repo containing my code for the web application can be found here.
